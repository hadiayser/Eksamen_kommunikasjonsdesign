The Unspoken Realities of The Red Nation's Tragedy, exclusive accounts from the bloodiest battle in World War Two, the battle of Stalingrad.


In a touching salute to the too often forgotten and overlooked human sacrifices of World War Two, the German historian Jochen Hellbeck complied 'The Stalingrad Protocols', which are interviews with previous Soviet soldiers who lived and experienced the ravaging conflicts, giving us a look into the gut-wrenching staggering loss of life on the Eastern Front.

Among the estimated 27 million Soviet lives lost during World War Two, 8.7 million of those lost were military personnel, and the remaining were 19 million innocent civilians, people not involved in the cruelties of WW2. The battle of Stalingrad stands as a horrifying example of the personal and individual tragedies that unfolded across the Eastern Front.

As the bloody Battle of Stalingrad drew to a close on the last day of January 1943, the death toll was estimated to be 1.2 million people. 1.1 million of the casualties were Soviets, whereas 100.000 were Germans. 'The Stalingrad Protocols', include an astonishing number of first-hand interviews by Red Army soldiers. One of those interviewed, Vasily Zaytsev, who was the Red Army's top sniper, and claimed to have shot dead 242 Germans, said, «You often have to remember, and memory has a powerful impact,» he recalled one year after the battle, before the term PTSD had been invented, "Now I have unsteady nerves and I'm constantly shaking.".

These records are a testament to the resilience and sacrifice of a nation. As we remember the 75 million lives lost globally in WW2, the interviews from Stalingrad soldiers reminds us that each and every statistic represents individuals of a generation forever marked by the horrifying and profound cost of war.
