Introduction: Setting the Stage

Norway is looked up as a socially inclusive nation — a bastion of progressiveness, equality, wealth and human rights. This is all true, yet behind this image, hides a dark shadow with sharp claws burrowed deep, a reality: the continuous presence of racial prejudice. On the surface, Norway projects and image of a society in harmony, a country of breathtaking fjords and emerald-green forests that echo with tales of inclusivity and acceptance. Often portrayed as a progressive and perfect utopia because of its strong social welfare system, its approach to environmental challenges, and continues involvement in international peacekeeping missions. However, like everything else, peeling back the layers reveals discrimination, racism, inequality and more.

The Landscape of Racism in Norway

According to numbers from Statistisk sentralbyrå/Statistics Norway (SSB) (ha med link her) one in four immigrants experienced discrimination because of their ethnic background. Compared to the general population, immigrants faced a much higher rate of prejudice in the past year. Compared to 19% of the general population, 50% of Norwegians born to immigrant parents and 40% of immigrants have faced discrimination. Nearly one in four immigrants report having encountered discrimination based on their ethnic heritage in the last year. Additionally, 12% of respondents claim to have experienced discrimination due to their skin tone, and 7% cite their religion or way of life as the reason.

This chart helps us see how common racism is in our society among different groups of people. The chart draws attention to the startlingly high rates at which people have experienced racism on the basis of their religion, race, or ethnicity. Significant obstacles are faced by immigrants, as evidenced by the 24.2% of those who describe encounters with ethnicity, 12.2% with skin color, and 7.1% with religion. The percentages for the Norwegian-born students with immigrant parents are 31.3%, 24.4%, and 15.6%, respectively, indicating that their challenges are still present. The chart also shows the need for more public awareness, since even in the general population, 1.3%, 1%, and 1% of respondents say they have experienced prejudice because of their religion, race, or ethnicity, respectively. These percentages highlight how urgent it is to create a society that is really inclusive and free from discriminatory attitudes and behaviors.

Looking at these numbers highlights how awfully common racist discrimination is in Norway, and it puts into perspective just how many people are affected, friends, family or yourself even. Most of those who experience racist discrimination can move past it, but that's not the case for everyone, most hauntingly Benjamin Hermansen, a young boy, only 15 years of age, who in 2001, whose life was violently and disgustingly taken away by two self-proclaimed nazis whose only goal was to murder innocents people solely based on the colour of their skin.

Benjamin Hermansen: The Symbolic Figure

Delving into the life of Benjamin Hermansen, we unravel a narrative that encapsulates the profound impact of racism in Norway. Benjamin born to a Ghanian father and a Norwegian mother in 1985, reflected the multicultural fabric that Norway was becoming. Benjamin was raised in Oslo, more specifically the neighbourhood of Holmlia. Benjamin embodied diversity and served as a symbol of the country's changing identity.

Tragically, at the young age of 15, Benjamin Hermansen life was cut short. The circumstances surrounding his murder in 2001 were fuelled by an unsettling manifestation of neo-Nazi ideology. Benjamin and his friend were on their way home late at night. Then a car with the assailants — Joe Erling Jahr, Ole Nicolai Kvisle and Veronica Andreassen, stops right beside them. According to this news article from VG ,this is how the conversation between Benjamin and his friend went. - “Are you thinking the same as me” Benjamin asked his friend. — “Yes, neo-nazis”, his friend answered back. The friends split up, and that was when Benjamin was attacked and fatally stabbed the assailants driven by racial hatred. This horrific act of violence shocked the nation to its core and exposed the terrible reality that people of diverse backgrounds experience. (skrive mer senere?)

We turn to those who knew Benjamin Hermansen the best — his friends, family and members of his community. — “Benjamin died as a hero, don't think about him like a victim,” his best friend Victor Lopez said. Hermansen's family, grappling with the unimaginable loss brought on by racial prejudice shared their grief and the enduring pain. Friends spoke about special times spent with him, praising his integrity, generosity, and potential. The community also echoed a sense of shared grief and resolve to face the ghost of prejudice that had taken one of their own lives. A personal perspective emerges, painting a clear image of a young man with dreams, aspirations and an unbreakable spirit.

Following Benjamin Hermansen's murder, there was a significant shift in the national conversation around racism in Norway. The shockwaves from this tragedy reverberated across the nation, prompting introspection and a collective call to action. The incident laid bare a hard reality by exposing racial prejudices that had previously been hidden beneath the surface.

Benjamin Hermansen's passing served as a catalyst for change, igniting projects, discussions, and a heightened consciousness of the pernicious effects of racism. His passing mobilized individuals to work towards creating a more inclusive and tolerant society. Hermansen's passing turned him into a symbol, a person whose legacy serves as a stark reminder of the pressing need for social reflection, dialogue, and the dismantling of systems that uphold racial injustice.

Students Speak Out: Racism In The Educating System

For this news story, I, Hadi Ayser Ali, decided to interview two student I know who have experienced racism in the classroom before. Both our students wanted to remain anonymous, so with their permission we decided to use the names “Ahmed” and “Richie” respectively.

Ahmed shared with us a troubling incident of racism he encountered with one of his teachers in high school. Ahmed recalled a grading exam that went well until the teacher, who was very impressed with Ahmed's English proficiency, and asked where Ahmed has learned to speak English so well. Ahmed answered by saying, “I lived in Kenya for 12 years, that's where I learnt”. The conversation took an unexpected turn when the teacher immediately assumed that Ahmed had lived in a refugee camp. Ahmed clarified that he had lived in a house, and emphasizing to me that, " all the students from a minority background had to work twice as hard to get a grade the same or even lower as the other student.”

Ahmed stressed that educational institutions perform more thorough background checks on those they hire, and he emphasized the value of instructors keeping an open mind.

Richie, the other student I interviewed, talked about her experience with racism with their social studies teacher in high school. Richie, who has done exceptionally well in their social studies class, felt singled out because of their foreign sounding last name. Richie related an instance in which the teacher called her derogatory terms like "ape". Richie's grades in this class were negatively impacted by this harassment, and even though Richie's grades were consistently high during the semester, the social studies teacher ultimately gave Richie a much lower grade on the rapport card than was deserved.

Richie asks that people avoid voicing racist opinions and stressed the importance of having an open mind.

A Call to Action and Initiatives

Benjamin Hermansen's tragic murder is an example of where the cruelest and most extremt thoughts and actions of racism and discrimination can led to. Norway witnessed a surge of initiatives aimed at addressing the deep-seated issues of racism and discrimination within the nation. An important initiative that gained traction was greater emphasis on cultural diversity education.

On the milder side we have students like "Ahmed" and "Richie", who have experienced racism and or discrimination in educational institutions. These are often remarks or comments given by teachers, but as we see, they often go beyond, and try to put less value, or ruin a student's grades.

Expreiences like this are unfortunately a too often of an occurrence, but to Norway's credit the nation and its people have demonstrated a resolute commitment to transforming its narrative and fostering an environment where diversity is embraced rather than marginalized

Conclusion: Toward a More Inclusive Future

Yet, the journey is far from over. The tale of Benjamin Hermansen serves as a catalyst for change and a constant reminder that the fight against racism is ongoing. It takes a persistent commitment to envision a more inclusive future—a dedication that transcends laws and penetrates the very fabric of society.

In this ongoing narrative, conversation turns into a potent catalyst for understanding. Informed by a shared commitment, discussions regarding race and identity the threads weaving a tapestry of inclusivity. The legacy of Benjamin Hermansen, and the experiences of students like Ahmed and Richie is a lesson for the nation, steering Norway in the direction of a future in which each person can flourish in an environment of true equality.
